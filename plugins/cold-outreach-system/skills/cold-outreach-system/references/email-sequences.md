# Email Sequence Design & Frameworks

## Sequence Length by Target

| Target Type | Emails | Duration | Spacing |
|-------------|--------|----------|---------|
| SMB (< 200 employees) | 4-5 | 14-21 days | Days 1, 3, 7, 10, 14 |
| Mid-Market (200-1000) | 5-7 | 21-30 days | Days 1, 3, 7, 10, 14, 21, 30 |
| Enterprise (1000+) | 7-10 | 45-60 days | Days 1, 3, 7, 14, 21, 30, 45, 60 |

## The 5-Email Sequence Structure

### Email 1: The Opener (Day 1)

**Goal**: Get a reply. That's it. Don't sell.

```
Subject: [specific, under 50 chars, no spam words]

[Personalized first line — trigger event, content reference, or observation]

[One sentence: the problem you solve, framed as their pain]

[One sentence: hint at your solution without explaining it fully]

[Interest-based CTA: "Worth exploring?" or "Is this on your radar?"]

[Name]
[Title]

Reply "stop" to opt out.
```

**Rules:**
- Under 75 words (target: 54)
- First line must be personalized (NEVER generic)
- CTA asks for interest, NOT a meeting
- No attachments, no images, no HTML formatting
- Plain text only (higher deliverability)

### Email 2: The Value Add (Day 3)

**Goal**: Provide value even if they don't buy.

```
Subject: Re: [original subject] OR [new value-driven subject]

[Name] — quick thought on [their situation].

[Share a relevant data point, insight, or resource]

[Connect it to their specific context]

[Soft CTA: "Thought this might be useful. Happy to share more if relevant."]

[Name]
```

**Rules:**
- Can reply to Email 1 thread OR new subject (test both)
- Give something valuable — a stat, article, framework, or insight
- Don't pitch. Build credibility.
- Even shorter than Email 1 (40-60 words)

### Email 3: The Social Proof (Day 7)

**Goal**: Build credibility with proof.

```
Subject: Re: [thread] OR [result-driven subject]

[Name] — wanted to share a quick result.

[Specific case study or metric: "We helped [similar company] achieve [result] in [timeframe]."]

[Connect to their situation: "Given [their context], thought this might be relevant."]

[CTA: "Curious if [their company] is dealing with something similar?"]

[Name]
```

**Rules:**
- Use the most relevant case study to their industry/size
- Specific numbers beat vague claims ("47% increase" > "significant improvement")
- If no case study exists, use a testimonial quote or industry stat

### Email 4: The Alternative Angle (Day 10-14)

**Goal**: Try a different frame in case the first didn't resonate.

```
Subject: [completely different angle]

[Name] — different approach to [topic].

[Reframe the problem from a new angle]
[Maybe it's not about [original angle] — maybe it's about [new angle].]

[New CTA: try a different format]
  — "Would a 2-min video walkthrough be more useful?"
  — "I put together a quick [checklist/comparison/analysis] — want me to send it?"

[Name]
```

**Rules:**
- Don't repeat the same pitch
- Change the angle, tone, or CTA format
- This is where you test if the first angle was wrong

### Email 5: The Breakup (Day 14-21)

**Goal**: Get a response through loss aversion or clean exit.

```
Subject: closing the loop

[Name] — I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back. No worries.

[One line: what they'd miss — loss aversion, not pressure]
  — "Just wanted to make sure you didn't miss [specific value]."

[Clean exit: "If the timing isn't right, totally understand.
I'll close this loop unless I hear otherwise."]

[Name]

Reply "stop" and I'll remove you from future messages.
```

**Rules:**
- Lowercase subject line ("closing the loop" not "FINAL OFFER")
- No guilt-tripping, no passive aggression
- The breakup email often gets the highest reply rate (15-20%)
- Always include opt-out

## Email Copy Frameworks

### PAS (Problem — Agitate — Solution)
Highest converting for pain-driven products.

```
[Personalized first line]

[PROBLEM: Name the exact pain]
"Most [persona] spend [X hours/dollars] on [painful task]."

[AGITATE: Show the cost of inaction]
"That's [consequence] every [time period]."

[SOLUTION: Hint at your answer]
"[Product] cuts that to [better outcome] — [social proof]."

[CTA]: "Worth a look?"
```

### QVC (Question — Value — CTA)
Fastest framework. Best for short emails.

```
[Personalized first line]

[QUESTION: Provoke thought about their problem]
"How much time does your team spend on [painful task]?"

[VALUE: One-line value prop]
"[Product] helps [similar companies] [achieve outcome]."

[CTA]: "Is this something you're thinking about?"
```

### BAB (Before — After — Bridge)
Best for transformation-focused products.

```
[Personalized first line]

[BEFORE: Their current painful reality]
"Right now, [their situation] means [pain]."

[AFTER: The desired state]
"Imagine [desired outcome] in [timeframe]."

[BRIDGE: Your product connects the two]
"[Product] makes this possible by [mechanism]."

[CTA]: "Want to see how?"
```

### The Goated One-Liner
Ultra-short. For senior executives who won't read more than 2 lines.

```
Subject: [their company] + [your value]

[Name] — [one sentence combining personalization + value prop + CTA].

[Name]
```

Example:
```
Subject: Acme + faster CI

Sarah — saw Acme's hiring 3 DevOps engineers. If slow pipelines are the bottleneck, we cut build times by 60% for teams your size. Worth a quick look?

James
```

## Subject Line Formulas

### What Works (with open rate data)

| Formula | Open Rate | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Question format | 46% | "Struggling with [problem]?" |
| "Hi {{first_name}}" | 45% | "Hi Sarah" |
| Name + company | 43% | "Sarah — quick thought on Acme" |
| Lowercase, casual | 42% | "quick question about [topic]" |
| Specific outcome | 40% | "[Company] + 60% faster [outcome]" |

### What Kills Open Rates

| Trigger | Why |
|---------|-----|
| ALL CAPS | Screams spam |
| "FREE", "URGENT", "Act Now" | Spam filter triggers |
| Exclamation marks!!! | Unprofessional |
| "Re:" when it's not a reply | Deceptive, breaks trust |
| Over 50 characters | Gets cut off on mobile |
| Your company name in subject | Nobody cares about your brand (yet) |

### Subject Line Rules

- Under 50 characters (mobile-friendly)
- Lowercase preferred (casual, personal feel)
- No spam trigger words
- Specific > vague ("CI pipeline speed" > "productivity")
- Curiosity or relevance — never clickbait
- Test 2-3 subject lines per sequence

## CTA Patterns

### Interest-Based CTAs (2x better reply rate)

```
"Is this on your radar?"
"Worth exploring?"
"Curious if this resonates?"
"Is [problem] something you're dealing with?"
"Would it be useful to see how [similar company] handled this?"
"Open to learning more?"
```

### Meeting-Request CTAs (use sparingly)

```
"Would a 15-minute walkthrough be useful?"
"Open to a quick call this week?"
"Want me to show you how it works?"
```

**Rule**: Use interest-based CTAs for Emails 1-3. Only use meeting requests in Email 4+ when they've shown interest.

### CTAs to NEVER Use

```
"Does Tuesday at 2pm work?" — Presumptuous, no relationship earned
"Let's hop on a quick call" — "Quick call" is never quick
"Can I have 30 minutes of your time?" — Too much to ask cold
"I'd love to pick your brain" — You're the one selling, not asking favors
"Let me know your thoughts" — Too vague, easy to ignore
```

## Benchmarks (2025-2026)

| Metric | Poor | Average | Good | Great |
|--------|------|---------|------|-------|
| Open rate | < 30% | 30-45% | 45-60% | 60%+ |
| Reply rate | < 2% | 2-5% | 5-10% | 10%+ |
| Positive reply rate | < 1% | 1-3% | 3-5% | 5%+ |
| Meeting book rate | < 0.5% | 0.5-1% | 1-3% | 3%+ |

### Reply Rate by Email Number

| Email | Typical Reply Rate | Cumulative |
|-------|-------------------|------------|
| Email 1 | 5-8% | 5-8% |

| Email 3 | 4-6% | 12-19% |
| Email 4 | 2-3% | 14-22% |
| Email 5 (breakup) | 5-7% | 19-29% |

The breakup email often matches or exceeds Email 1's reply rate.

## Optimal Send Times

| Day | Time (ET) | Quality |
|-----|-----------|---------|
| Tuesday | 8-10am | Best |
| Wednesday | 8-10am | Best |
| Thursday | 8-10am | Good |
| Monday | 9-11am | Good |
| Friday | 8-9am only | Acceptable |
| Sat/Sun | — | Never |

- Send in the recipient's timezone (not yours)
- Avoid sending at exactly :00 or :30 (looks automated)
- Stagger sends (not all at once — looks like a blast)
